Children with disability: play & friends

Play and friendship for children with disability

Play and friendship are good for children with disability. Here’s how to help your child with disability play, make friends and build social skills.

Children with disability: activity guides

Mirroring movement: activity for children with disability or autism

In a mirroring movement game, you copy movements exactly. This play activity builds coordination and motor skills for children with disability or autism.

Sand tray play: activity for children with disability or autism

Sand tray play can be a rich sensory experience for children with disability or autism. You need a large tray, sand, rice or dirt and some small objects.

Fabric play: activity for children with disability or autism

Fabric play helps children with disability or autism build imaginative and motor skills. You need fabrics of different lengths, colours and textures.

Roll-a-story: activity for children with disability or autism

In roll-a-story, you use dice to decide where a story goes. This can build communication and numeracy skills for children with disability or autism.

Shared drawing: activity for children with disability or autism

In shared drawing, you make a picture together by taking turns to add to it. This helps children with disability or autism learn to cooperate and share.

‘What’s that sound?’ Activity for children with disability or autism

In What’s that sound? children guess an everyday object by the sound it makes. It can help children with disability or autism learn to take turns and focus.

Chalk racetrack: activities for children with disability or autism

Chalk racetrack activities help children with disability or autism build gross motor skills and fitness, and learn numeracy concepts like time and speed.

Nature doll: activity for children with disability or autism

You can use sticks, seedpods and leaves to make a nature doll. This activity encourages children with disability and autism to be creative and imaginative.

Torch light play: activity for children with disability or autism

Torch light play encourages children with disability or autism to use their imaginations and senses. All you need is a torch to make light and shadows.

Ball run: activity for children with disability or autism

Ball runs can help children with disability or autism build motor, problem-solving and cooperation skills. You just need recycled cardboard and some balls.

Children with disability: learning & behaviour

Helping children with disability learn through everyday interests and play

Everyday play and activities can help children with disability learn new things. You can spark children’s motivation to learn by tuning into their interests.

Teaching skills to children with disability: practical strategies

Practical strategies for teaching skills to children with disability include teaching by telling, teaching by showing, and teaching step by step. Read more.

Children with disability: child care

Child care for children with disability, autism or other additional needs: services, inclusion and funding

Children with disability have the right to use child care. It’s also good for their development. Services should be safe, engaging, accessible and inclusive.

Starting child care: children with disability, autism or other additional needs

An individual education plan, visits, meetings with educators and professional support can help children with disability make a positive start at child care.

Children with disability: school

Choosing primary schools for children with disability

When choosing primary schools for children with disability, look at distance, cost, OOSH care, accessibility, learning support and disability policies.

Primary school options for children with disability

Primary school options for children with disability include mainstream and specialist schools. Your child has the same educational rights as all children.

Enrolling and starting primary school: children with disability

When children with disability are enrolling and starting school, it’s best to plan and work with school staff on supports and adjustments for your child.

School support for children with disability

Government, independent and Catholic schools all have school support and funding for children with disability. Ask your child’s school for more information.

Being an advocate for children with disability, autism or other additional needs

Children with disability might need you to be an advocate for them. Advocating is speaking up for your child’s needs, interests and right to be included.

Starting secondary school: children with disability

Children with disability starting secondary school might need extra support, including a transition plan, student support group and individual learning plan.

Choosing secondary schools for children with disability

For children with disability, start thinking about secondary schools in Year 5. Visit secondary schools to find out how they support and include children.

Disability and education state and territory agencies

If you have a child with disability going to school, you might need to contact your state or territory disability and education agencies. Get details here.

Children with disability: work

Employment and jobs: teenagers with disability, autism or other additional needs

You can help teens with disability, autism or other additional needs prepare for jobs. Start by identifying your child’s strengths then set employment goals.

Employment rights: teenagers with disability, autism or other additional needs

Young people with disability, autism or other needs have employment rights. They’re also entitled to some workplace adjustments so they can do the job well.

Employment services and resources: teenagers with disability, autism or other additional needs

Many services, programs and resources can help young people with disability and autism prepare for jobs and employment. This page has key links and contacts.

Volunteering: teenagers with disability, autism or other additional needs

Volunteering can help teens with disability or other needs build skills and confidence. Ask about volunteer work in the community or at volunteer agencies.
