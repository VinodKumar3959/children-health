The NDIS and children: FAQs

The NDIS: reasonable and necessary supports

The NDIS funds reasonable and necessary supports for children with developmental delay and disability. These supports help children work towards their goals.

NDIS terminology: a guide

When your child is using the NDIS, it helps to understand key NDIS terminology. Our guide explains the NDIS terms that parents and children need to know.

The NDIS and early intervention: the early childhood approach

The NDIS aims to make sure that children with developmental delay or disability get quick access to early childhood early intervention. Read more.

Quality in early childhood intervention

Quality early childhood intervention services are family centred. They help families develop skills to support children through everyday learning activities.

NDIS support pathways are different for children under 7 years and children 7 years and older. Both pathways aim to give children quick access to support.

NDIS access: unsuccessful requests

NDIS access requests: when your child’s request is unsuccessful

NDIS access requests might be unsuccessful for various reasons. If your child’s NDIS access request is unsuccessful, you can apply again or request a review.

NDIS access requests and plans: when you want a review

If your child’s NDIS access request is rejected or you disagree with your child’s NDIS plan, you can ask for a review. You must ask within 3 months.

NDIS plans

Your child’s NDIS plan: developing and getting a plan

Your child’s NDIS plan says what your child needs and what supports the NDIS will give. The planning process has several steps, including a planning meeting.

Your child’s NDIS goals: how to develop goals

NDIS goals are things you want your child to achieve with NDIS support. Setting goals and linking them to supports is central to developing an NDIS plan.

Your child’s NDIS plan: what to do if your circumstances change

If your child has an NDIS plan and your circumstances change, you must let the NDIA know. The NDIA will tell you whether the plan needs to be reviewed.

Your child’s NDIS plan: regular plan reviews

NDIS plans are regularly reviewed. A plan review is a good chance to assess your child’s goals and support. After the review, your child will get a new plan.

Your child’s NDIS plan: when your child turns 18

When NDIS participants turn 18, they’re encouraged to manage their NDIS plans. Early preparation and support can help children build skills to manage plans.

NDIS budgets & plan management

Your child’s NDIS funds: options for managing them

There are 3 options for managing your child’s NDIS funds: self-managing, plan-managing and NDIA-managing. You can choose which option suits you best.

Your child’s NDIS support budgets: how they work

NDIS plans have 3 budgets – core supports, capacity building supports and capital supports. Budget funding must help your child reach their NDIS goals.

Self-managed NDIS funds: tips for parents

NDIS self-management gives you flexibility in using NDIS funds. You’ll need to organise and keep records and consider how to engage NDIS support workers.

Self-managed NDIS funds: employing support workers

If you’re self-managing your child’s NDIS funds, you can employ NDIS support workers. You’ll need to consider a job description, pay, super, tax and more.

The National Disability Insurance Scheme: services and support

Disability services: a guide for children with disability, autism or other additional needs

Disability services provide intervention, therapies and supports to help children with disability, autism, developmental delay or other additional needs.

Choosing service providers for children with disability, autism or other additional needs

The best disability service providers for children with disability, autism or other additional needs are the ones that meet your child’s and family’s needs.

NDIS providers: registered and unregistered

NDIS providers supply NDIS services and supports. Providers can be registered or unregistered. This affects who can use them but shouldn’t affect quality.

NDIS providers: quality, respect and safety

NDIS providers should offer good-quality, respectful and safe services and supports. If you’re unhappy with an NDIS provider or service, it’s OK to say so.

NDIS support and services for rural and remote families: tips

If you have a child with disability or autism and live in rural and remote Australia, our tips can help you get NDIS support. Access to NDIS services is key.

NDIS services and LGBTQ+ families: cultural safety and inclusion

The NDIS is for all Australians with disability and their families. If your family is LGBTQ+, your NDIS experiences should be culturally safe and inclusive.

NDIS services and LGBTQ+ families: sharing information about your family

You can decide what information to share with the NDIS about your LGBTQ+ family. It might help to consider what the NDIS needs to know to support your child.

NDIS services and LGBTQ+ families: service providers in your home

Your LGBTQ+ family or child has the right to respectful NDIS services in your own home. It’s OK to check on service providers’ knowledge of LGBTQ+ families.

Complaints about NDIS providers

If you’re unhappy with an NDIS provider, you can complain. Start by complaining to the NDIS provider. If that doesn’t work, complain to the NDIS Commission.

Support for children with disability: not funded by the NDIS

The NDIS doesn’t fund disability supports that children can get from community, government and other services. This guide outlines non NDIS-funded support.
