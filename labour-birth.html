Pregnancy: labour & birth

Birth environment

Public hospitals: pregnancy care and birth

Public hospitals are safe options for pregnancy care and birth. Midwives usually look after women having public hospital births. Medicare covers most costs.

Private hospitals: pregnancy care and birth

Private hospitals offer comfortable, caring birth environments. Private obstetricians look after pregnancy care for women having a private hospital births.

Birth centres: pregnancy care and birth

Birth centres support healthy women with low-risk pregnancies to give birth in a home-like environment. Pregnancy care at birth centres is led by midwives.

Homebirth: pregnancy care and birth

Homebirth is an option for healthy women with low-risk pregnancies who can transfer to hospital. Private midwives care for homebirthing women in pregnancy.

Preparing for birth

Labour and birth: getting ready

Planning for labour and birth can help you feel ready. Consider birth classes and a birth plan. Packing a hospital bag and stocking your freezer are good too.

When your baby is overdue

Overdue baby? If labour hasn’t started by 41-42 weeks of pregnancy, you and baby will have more check-ups, and you might need to decide about being induced.

Vaginal & caesarean birth

Labour and birth: what to expect

Early labour signs include a show, waters breaking and pain. During labour, your contractions increase and your cervix dilates, so you can birth your baby.

Vaginal birth: a guide

A vaginal birth is often the safest. Continuity of pregnancy care, birth classes, a birth plan and a calm birth environment make vaginal birth more likely.

Pain relief in labour

Pain relief in labour includes natural options like massage, as well as medical options. It’s best to discuss options with a midwife or doctor in pregnancy.

Support people: labour and birth

Support people encourage you during labour and birth. The right support can help labour progress. Check with birth settings on rules about support people.

Caesarean birth: a guide to planned and unplanned caesarean

A caesarean is sometimes the safest way to give birth. It might be planned or unplanned. It has some medical risks and usually means a longer hospital stay.

Planned caesarean birth: what to expect

You might have an elective caesarean because of health or pregnancy problems. It involves major surgery, but you can usually see your baby straight after.

Vaginal birth after caesarean (VBAC)

For many birthing mothers, vaginal birth after caesarean – VBAC – is a safe and positive way to have a baby. Our guide explains VBAC’s benefits and risks.

Recovery after birth

Recovery after caesarean: first 6 weeks

This guide to recovery after caesarean section has tips for wound care, pain relief, practical help, physical and emotional recovery, and breastfeeding.

Recovery after birth: in pictures

For recovery after birth, try these tips: get plenty of rest, support your back when breastfeeding, avoid heavy lifting, and return to exercise slowly.

Birthing mothers: your body after birth

New mums, if you’ve just given birth, your body has been through a lot. Find out about vaginal bleeding, afterpains, nipples, breasts, continence and weight.

Birthing mothers: the first weeks

If you’ve just given birth and are a new mum, rest and focus on getting to know your baby. It’s also good to get support in the first weeks with your baby.
