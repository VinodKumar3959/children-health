Family routines: how and why they work

Daily routines are how families organise themselves, spend time together and have fun. Routines help children feel safe. They also reduce parent stress.

Family rituals: what are they?

Family rituals are special things you do regularly as a family. They strengthen family bonds and they can help children feel secure in uncertain times.

Family rules

Family rules are good for the whole family. This guide explains how to involve children and teens in making rules and get rules working well for your family.

Household chores for children

When children do household chores they learn skills and contribute to family life. Chores for kids include tidying up, setting the table, cooking and more.

Pocket money: children and teenagers

Pocket money can help children learn the basics of managing money. But your decisions about pocket money will depend on your family circumstances and values.

Money management for children

Money management is a key life skill. You can introduce children to what money looks like and help them learn about needs, wants, budgets, saving and value.

Money management for teenagers

Help teens learn to manage money by modelling money management, letting them help with household finances, and giving them responsibility for their own money.

Managing money and budgeting

Basic money management is about meeting family expenses, setting money aside for emergencies, and saving. A family budget helps you spend and save wisely.

Managing money and avoiding debt

Managing money will help you avoid debt, feel financially secure, reduce general stress and enjoy family life. Avoid debt by spending less than you earn.

Family violence: what to do if you’re experiencing it

If you’re experiencing family violence, it harms you, your children and your ability to be the parent you want to be. Getting help is the best thing to do.

Grown-ups: child sexual abuse

Child sexual abuse: what it is and what to do

Child sexual abuse is when adults or older children involve children in sexual activity. Sexual abuse is never a child’s fault. Read how to protect children.

Child sexual abuse: talking to children 0-11 years

Talking about child sexual abuse helps protect children aged 0-11 years. You can talk about feeling unsafe, inappropriate touch, secrets and saying ‘no’.

Child sexual abuse: talking to teenagers

Talking about child sexual abuse helps teens stay safe and understand what abuse looks like. It’s important to talk about consent, respect and saying no.

Preventing child sexual abuse in your family and community

Preventing child sexual abuse starts with your family and community. Prevent abuse by developing family safety rules and working with local organisations.

Signs of sexual abuse in children and teenagers

Children and teenagers might not tell you about sexual abuse. Instead you might notice signs of sexual abuse, like physical, emotional or behaviour changes.

Signs that someone is involved in child sexual abuse

People who sexually abuse children are likely to be people you know. Signs include buying a child expensive gifts or trying to spend time alone with a child.

Grooming: recognising the signs

Grooming is when a person gets friendly with a child or family for later sexual abuse. Signs include a person taking special interest in a family or child.

Child sexual abuse: what to do if a child is sexually abused

If a child tells you about sexual abuse, stay calm, let the child talk, believe the child and help the child feel safe. Report abuse – call police on 000.

Child sexual abuse: help and support for children

Children who have experienced sexual abuse can heal with professional support. Love, reassurance, routines, family time, and talking and listening also help.

Child sexual abuse: helplines and services

If children or teens have experienced child sexual abuse, call police on 000. You can also call state or territory child protection services. Get contacts.

Supporting children who have engaged in harmful sexual behaviour with other children

Harmful sexual behaviour in children can take many forms. Professional support is essential to helping children who have engaged in harmful sexual behaviour.

Child sexual abuse in autistic children and teenagers: recognising, responding and reporting

Child sexual abuse signs in autistic children and teens include emotional or behaviour changes and physical injuries. Tell the police about abuse – call 000.

Child sexual abuse: keeping autistic children and teenagers safe

Protect autistic children and teens from sexual abuse by teaching them about public and private body parts, behaviour and places, and also unsafe situations.
