Autism: communication & relationships

Autism: communicating

Communication: autistic children

You can work step by step on communication skills with autistic children. Start by assessing what level of communication your child is using right now.

Conversation skills for autistic pre-teens and teenagers

You can help autistic teenagers build conversation skills with a step-by-step approach. Role-play and visual supports can help them practise these skills.

Paying attention: autistic children and teenagers

Here are five strategies to help autistic children with paying attention: interesting activities, effective instructions, talk, modelling and transitions.

Augmentative and alternative communication: autistic children

Autistic children can have trouble with communication. An augmentative and alternative communication system might help your autistic child communicate.

Autism: family relationships

Positive relationships in families with autistic children

Positive family relationships are important for families with autistic children. You can build individual relationships while focusing on family strengths.

Stress and stress management in families with autistic children

Families with autistic children often have a lot of stress. Stress management tips include positive thinking, family activites, getting organised and respite.

Siblings of autistic children: experiences, relationships and support

Strengthen relationships between autistic children and siblings by making special time for siblings, finding ways for siblings to have fun, and being fair.

Parent feelings: children with disability, autism or other additional needs

If children get a diagnosis of disability, autism or other additional needs, it’s common for parents to have a range of feelings. It’s good to get support.

Parent relationships: children with disability, autism or other additional needs

There are great things about raising children with disability, autism or other additional needs. If you’re in a relationship, it can also bring challenges.

Family travel and children with disability, autism or other additional needs

Travel with kids with disability, autism or additional needs can take careful planning. Consider medication, equipment and strategies to prepare your child.

Extended family and friends: supporting families with autistic children

Extended family and friends can offer great support to families with autistic children. The key is helping people understand how autism affects your child.

Autism: social relationships

Friends and peers: autistic children and teenagers

With help, autistic children and teenagers can learn to make and keep friends. Start by helping them meet and socialise with peers who share their interests.

Circle of friends: personal boundaries activity for children 3-15 years

A circle of friends activity shows different people in a child’s life. It helps children learn about personal boundaries and stay safe from sexual abuse.

Romantic relationships and feelings: autistic teenagers

Visual supports, social stories and talking can help autistic teenagers recognise attraction and negotiate romantic relationships, respect and sensory issues.

Social skills for autistic children

Social skills are important for friendships, learning and more. Strategies to help autistic children include role-play, video-modelling and social stories.

Social skills for autistic pre-teens and teenagers

You can help autistic teenagers develop social skills with strategies like role-play, video-modelling, social stories, prompt cards, social groups and more.

Autism: coping with traumatic events

Traumatic events: first response to help autistic children

Traumatic events are sudden and shocking. Autistic children might need special support to feel safe, calm and grounded and to understand what’s going on.

Traumatic events: supporting autistic children in the days and weeks afterwards

In the days and weeks after traumatic events, autistic children might need special support. Explain what’s happening, use routines and work on emotions.

Grounding: a calming exercise for children and teenagers

Grounding exercises help children calm down after distressing experiences. In a grounding exercise, children breathe deeply and focus on things around them.
