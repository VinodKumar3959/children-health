Preschoolers: play & development

Why play is important

Emotions and play: preschoolers

Preschoolers are getting better at expressing emotions. Play ideas to develop preschooler emotions include sand play, dress-ups, music, drawing and reading.

Learning about emotions: activity for children 3-6 years

Learning about emotions helps children feel happier and cope better. This activity helps children practise have fun recognising and understanding emotions.

Imagining, creating and play: preschoolers

Play fosters imagination in preschoolers, which is important for development. Play ideas include nature walks, busy boxes, dress-ups, puppet play and more.

Movement and play: preschoolers

Play is key to motor skills development. Play ideas to get preschoolers moving include ball play, outdoor play, nature walks, and rough-and-tumble play.

Talking and play: preschoolers

Preschoolers love to chat! It’s a key part of building their vocabularies. Play ideas to develop preschooler talking include signing, reading and rhyming.

Thinking and play: preschoolers

Preschoolers’ brains are hard at work when they’re playing. Play ideas for cognitive development include reading, board and memory games, and outside play.

Puppet play and emotions: activity for children 3-6 years

Puppet play is a fun way for children to learn about emotions. Use any puppet or soft toy or make your own. Follow your child’s lead with puppet play.

Preschoolers: getting play started

Preschoolers at play

Playing by themselves and with others is how preschoolers learn, experiment and solve problems. Here are some fun preschool games and play ideas.

Letting your child lead play: activities for children 0-6 years

Child-led play is following your child’s lead and responding to what they say or do. It’s good because your child learns most when they’re engaged in play.

Toys and games for children

Toys and games for kids – what’s best? Finding the right toys and games is about matching play ideas to your child’s age, developmental stage and interests.

Choosing toys for children

The best toys for kids spark imagination, creativity and problem-solving skills. Open-ended toys are good for this. You can often make these toys at home.

Preschoolers: low-cost play ideas

‘I spy’: turn-taking and talking activity for children 3-6 years

You and your children can play the ‘I spy’ game anywhere. It involves turn-taking and talking, so it’s good for your children’s social and language skills.

Collage: activity for children 2-6 years

Collage is a simple craft activity. Children paste materials onto paper. Collage is great for fine motor skills and colour and texture awareness.

Homemade toys and free activities for children

A little imagination can turn ordinary items into homemade toys and free activities for kids. Get ideas for babies, toddlers, preschoolers and school kids.

Stacking and building games: children 3-6 years

Stacking and building games are fun and good for your child’s creative learning, problem-solving and numeracy skills. Use blocks, boxes, cushions and so on.

Playdough activities: children 3-6 years

Playdough is a fun sensory experience for kids. It’s good for creativity, muscle strength and fine motor skills too. Our guide has homemade playdough recipes.

Cubbyhouse activity: children 2-6 years

Cubbyhouses are great for imaginative play, creative thinking and problem-solving. You can make a cubbyhouse with things around your home. Here are ideas.

Guess the animal game: activity for children 1-6 years

Our guess the animal game gets children using imagination and thinking skills. It can be good exercise too. You and your child can play this game anywhere.

Dress-up games: activity for children 3-6 years

Dress-up games let your child act out different roles, explore ideas, develop imagination, and express emotions in a safe way. Here’s how to get started.

Family story book activity: children 3-6 years

A family story book fosters your child’s sense of belonging and security. And making a story book with you can help to develop your child’s literacy skills.

Playing with cardboard boxes: activities for children 2-6 years

Cardboard boxes are great for imaginative play. Your child can use them to make castles, shops, caves and more. Cardboard boxes also encourage physical play.

Memory game for kids: 3-6 years

Memory games for kids build thinking skills, concentration and persistence. For this homemade memory game, you need some small objects, a tray and a cloth.

Peg play: activity for children 2-4 years

Through peg play children can develop numeracy and motor skills and enjoy experimental play. All you need is plastic pegs and some other household items.

Preschoolers: outdoor play

Outdoor play

Outdoor play is a big part of growth, development and wellbeing for children. Outdoor play ideas include tummy time, ball play, nature walks and bike rides.

Noticing nature walk: activity for children 3-6 years

A noticing nature walk encourages your child to observe with all senses. All you need is somewhere to walk. Questions can prompt your child to notice things.

Listening walk: activity for children 4-6 years

On a listening walk, your child tries to focus on sounds. A listening walk helps young children learn to focus. It can also be an early mindfulness activity.

Preschoolers: active play

Physical activity for young children

Daily physical activity is good for babies, toddlers and preschoolers. When you make physical activity fun and part of family life, it’s easy to get moving.

Rough-and-tumble play: a guide

Rough play and play fighting can be fun. It can also help children develop social and physical skills. Ground rules can prevent children from getting hurt.

Playing with balls: activities for children 3-6 years

Playing with balls is fun. It’s also good for muscle development, hand-eye coordination and fitness. You can make your own balls with rolled socks or paper.

Treasure hunt: activity for children 3-6 years

An outdoor treasure hunt game encourages children to be active and to explore. You set up a treasure hunt by making and hiding clues as well as the treasure.

Rough-and-tumble play: activities for children 3 months-8 years

Rough-and-tumble play is climbing, wrestling, rolling around and play fighting. It’s fun and good exercise. Get ideas for rough-and-tumble play activities.

Sharing and learning to share

Learning to share can be a challenge for children, but it’s key to getting along with others. Practice, encouragement and sharing games can help kids learn.

Preschoolers: learning ideas

Learning in the baby to preschool years

Right from birth, children learn by playing and exploring. They learn best by actively engaging with their environments and trying many different activities.

Handwriting skills for children

For toddlers and preschoolers, handwriting starts with drawing with crayons and chalk. Older children learn formal handwriting at school. Read how to help.

Drawing, scribbling and writing: activities for children 3-6 years

Drawing, scribbling and writing lay the foundations for handwriting. It’s good for toddlers and preschoolers to have a lot of opportunities to draw and write.

Early numeracy skills: how to develop them

Children develop numeracy and maths skills from birth. Build early numeracy skills by counting, sorting, looking at shapes, singing number songs and more.

Counting activities: children 3-6 years

Counting is an early numeracy skill. You can do counting games anytime and anywhere. Try counting when you and your child go shopping. Get more ideas.

Growth chart: numeracy activity for children 3-6 years

A homemade growth chart is a great way to develop early numeracy skills. You and your child can measure heights on a wall or door, or make a paper chart.

Museum activities for children: 3-6 years

Museum activities can be engaging and fun for children. Look for a child-friendly museum near you, and follow your child’s lead as you explore the museum.

Preschoolers: literacy, reading & stories

Developing literacy

Literacy development is learning about sounds, words and language. Everyday activities like singing, talking, reading and play help children develop literacy.

Literacy activities for children

Want fun literacy activities for your child? Sing nursery rhymes, play ‘I spy’, scribble, draw and write together – and read, read, read! Get more tips.

Reading activities: children 2-4 years

Reading helps child development in many ways. Start with picture books that have rhyme and rhythm. Try to share at least one book with your child each day.

Library activities: children 3-6 years

Library visits foster a love of books and reading. Your child can borrow books for free, go to story time and more. Here’s how to make the most of libraries.

Looking for words: literacy activity for children 1-5 years

It’s easy to help children learn literacy skills. For example, looking for words while you’re shopping is a fun way to promote literacy development.

Alphabet search: road trip activity for children 3-6 years

Alphabet search is looking for letters of the alphabet. It’s a fun way to build early literacy skills and can keep children happy and engaged on road trips.

Reading and storytelling with babies and children

Reading and storytelling with babies and children promotes brain development, teaches children about language and emotions, and strengthens relationships.

Reading with preschoolers

Reading and storytelling helps children learn. Try to read daily. Preschoolers like alphabet, shape and counting books, funny stories and quirky characters.

Family story book activity: children 3-6 years

A family story book fosters your child’s sense of belonging and security. And making a story book with you can help to develop your child’s literacy skills.

Ebooks and children: 2-8 years

For children over two, well-designed ebooks can help with learning. Look for ebooks with engaging stories and interactive features that support the text.

The Let’s Read literacy program supports you to get reading with your children. Check out Let’s Read for tips on building children’s literacy skills.

Preschoolers: learning difficulties

Early literacy difficulties

Early literacy difficulties include problems with speaking, listening, knowing letters and sounding out words. Talk to a professional if you’re concerned.

Dyslexia

Dyslexia is a learning disorder. Children with dyslexia have trouble with reading and spelling. Support helps children with dyslexia achieve and succeed.
