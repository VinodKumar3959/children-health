Toddlers: nutrients

Vitamins and minerals

Children need vitamins and minerals for health and development. They can get vitamins and minerals by eating a variety of foods from the five food groups.

Vitamin D: what you need to know

Vitamin D helps bones grow, develop and stay strong. Children get most of their vitamin D from sunlight as well as a small amount from some foods.

Iodine: what you need to know

Children need iodine for growth, development and good health. They can get iodine from packaged bread, seafood, eggs, fruit and vegetables, meat and dairy.

Carbohydrates and GI

GI measures how quickly our bodies get energy from carbohydrates. Low-GI foods give steady, long-lasting energy. They can be good choices for children.

Healthy fats and unhealthy fats: the basics

Reduce unhealthy fats in your family’s diet and replace them with healthy fats. Healthy fats are in nuts, lean meat, vegetable oils, green leafy veg and more.

Toddlers: healthy eating habits

Healthy eating habits for children

Healthy eating habits for kids include healthy food and opportunities to eat it. Healthy eating routines and messages can help with overeating or undereating.

Vegetables: tips to encourage your child to eat more

Vegetables give children energy and can protect them against chronic diseases. Our tips can help you encourage children to eat 2-4½ serves of vegies each day.

Healthy drinks for kids and teenagers

What are healthy drinks for kids and teens? Water is best. Low-fat milk is OK. Soft drink, cordial and fruit juice have a lot of sugar and are best avoided.

Vegetarian diets for children and teenagers

Children and teens who choose vegetarian diets need to eat a wide variety of fresh foods to get enough protein, omega-3 fatty acids, iron and vitamin B12.

Eating away from home: children and teenagers

Healthy eating away from home starts with good food choices at home. Aim for balance, pack healthy snacks when you’re out and look for healthy menu options.

Toddlers: common food & eating concerns

Fussy eating

Many children are fussy eaters, and fussy eating is normal. Pleasant, regular mealtimes can help, as can giving your child a choice of healthy food options.

Messy eating

Messy eating is a normal part of children’s development. Be patient, cut food into small bits, praise tidy eating, and set a good example at family meals.

Weaning off breastfeeding: older children

Is it time for weaning for your toddler or preschooler? How you wean is up to you, but it helps to take things slowly and give your child cuddles and comfort.

Toddler not eating? Ideas and tips

Toddler not eating? That’s pretty normal. It can help to think of it this way: you decide what food, your child decides how much to eat. Read more tips.

Childhood obesity

Childhood obesity is when children have too much body fat. A family lifestyle with enough healthy food and physical activity helps children avoid obesity.

Weaning off the bottle

It’s good to wean babies off the bottle by 12 months. You can wean quickly or gradually. If babies get upset, a cup, extra comfort and quality time can help.

Toddlers: physical activity

Physical activity for young children

Daily physical activity is good for babies, toddlers and preschoolers. When you make physical activity fun and part of family life, it’s easy to get moving.

Physical activity for babies and children: why and how much

How much physical activity do children need for health and wellbeing? Children aged 1-5 years need 3 hours a day. For kids aged 5-18, it’s at least 1 hour.

Physical activity: getting children involved

Getting children involved in fun physical activity keeps them healthy and well. It starts with free time to be active and different activities to try.

Winter activities to keep children active

When it’s cold and wet outside, keep kids active with fun winter activities like kite-flying, puddle-jumping, indoor sock bowling or soccer and more.
