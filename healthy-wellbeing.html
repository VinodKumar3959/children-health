Pregnancy: health & wellbeing

Healthy pregnancy: you & baby

COVID-19 and pregnancy

Pregnant women are at greater risk from COVID-19. They should get vaccinated, take protective measures and get advice from their health professionals.

COVID-19 vaccination: pregnancy and breastfeeding

If you’re pregnant, planning a pregnancy or breastfeeding, COVID-19 vaccination is recommended. Find out more.

Healthy eating for pregnancy: in pictures

Healthy eating for pregnancy means lots of fruit, vegetables and foods with calcium, protein and iron. Avoid sugary, fatty foods, and drink plenty of water.

Exercise in pregnancy: for women

Light to moderate exercise in pregnancy is usually safe. It’s also good for you and your baby. Walking, swimming and stationary cycling are safe exercises.

Healthy pregnancy for women who are overweight

Being overweight can cause complications in pregnancy. You can reach a healthier weight with changes to diet and activity levels and weight management advice.

Health problems in pregnancy

Many pregnancy health problems are mild, but always call your doctor if you’re worried about symptoms. A healthy lifestyle can help you avoid health problems.

Stillbirth: reducing the risks

You can reduce risk of stillbirth by eating well and exercising, sleeping on your side, and seeking immediate medical help if your baby’s movements change.

Pelvic floor care: in pictures

Your pelvic floor holds your bladder, bowel and uterus in place, but pregnancy and birth can weaken it. Do pelvic floor exercises: squeeze, lift and hold.

Alcohol and pregnancy: in pictures

If you’re pregnant, it’s best not to drink alcohol. If you’re trying to get pregnant, avoid alcohol. If you need help to quit, talk to your GP or midwife.

Smoking and pregnancy: in pictures

Smoking makes it harder to get pregnant and harms your baby if you’re pregnant. For help to quit smoking, call Quitline on 137 848, or speak to your GP.

Pregnancy: mental health

Finding out you’re pregnant: your feelings

You might feel many things when you find out you’re pregnant. Planning can help you manage feelings positively. It’s good to get help for difficult feelings.

Antenatal depression: pregnant women and birthing mothers

If pregnant women and birthing mothers experience negative emotional changes that last longer than 2 weeks, it might be antenatal or postnatal depression.

Antenatal anxiety and postnatal anxiety

If you’re having symptoms of antenatal or postnatal (perinatal) anxiety that are hard to control and interfere with daily life, it’s important to get help.

Stress and pregnancy

Stress and pregnancy often go together because pregnancy can bring big changes. Stress management is important for your health and your baby’s. Get tips.

Pregnancy: tests & appointments

GPs, shared care and pregnancy

Shared care is when you have some pregnancy appointments with your GP and some at hospital. Shared care can be good if you feel comfortable with your GP.

Appointments during pregnancy

At antenatal appointments, your doctor or midwife keeps track of your health and your baby’s health during pregnancy. You can ask questions and get support.

Tests in pregnancy

In pregnancy, you’ll be offered blood tests, ultrasound scans, urine tests and the GBS test. Pregnancy tests identify health concerns for you and your baby.

Antenatal tests: chromosomal anomalies and other conditions

Antenatal tests can tell you if your baby has chromosomal anomalies or other conditions. Your health professional can help you make choices about these tests.

How to decide on antenatal tests for chromosomal anomalies and other conditions

Antenatal screening and diagnostic tests can pick up chromosomal anomalies that cause disability. It’s important to make informed choices about these tests.

When your unborn baby has chromosomal anomalies or disability

Finding out your unborn baby has chromosomal anomalies or disability is very upsetting. You can continue or terminate the pregnancy. There’s no right or wrong.

Teenage pregnancy: supporting teenagers expecting a baby

If your teenage child is expecting a baby, your child needs your support. Pregnant teenagers also need extra care during pregnancy and early parenting.

Fraternal twins, identical twins and other types of twins
