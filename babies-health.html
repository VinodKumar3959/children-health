Babies: hygiene & keeping clean

Hygiene and daily care for babies

Find out how to clean baby eyes, ears, teeth, umbilical cords and genitals in our illustrated guide to hygiene and daily care for newborns and babies.

Bathing a baby

Safety, comfort and fun are key when bathing a baby. Use warm water in a baby bath, big bath or shower. Make sure everything you need is within reach.

Fear of the bath: babies and toddlers

Some young children are afraid of the bath. If your child has a fear of baths, introduce baths slowly, help your child feel safe, and make bath time fun.

Baby genitals: care and cleaning

Keeping your baby’s genitals clean will help prevent infections. To clean baby genitals, use warm water and a cotton ball. You don’t need soap. Read more.

Cleaning baby eyes, nose and ears

Baby eyes, baby ears and baby noses all need gentle cleaning once a day. Clean them with warm water and a cotton ball when you’re giving your baby a bath.

Trimming baby nails

Cutting baby nails is important, because short nails mean babies can’t scratch themselves. It can help to cut baby nails when baby is calm, drowsy or asleep.

Babies: poos, wees & nappies

Poos and wees

Looking at your baby’s poo and wee might sound disgusting, but it’s not. It’s actually one of the best ways to check your baby’s health. Find out more.

Constipation

Children with constipation have hard poo that’s difficult to push out. A high-fibre diet and regular toileting usually helps. Some children need laxatives.

Diarrhoea

Diarrhoea is large, runny, frequent or watery poo. It’s common in children. Make sure your child has enough fluids to drink as they recover from diarrhoea.

Nappies: cloth nappies and disposable nappies

What nappies should you use for your baby? Our guide takes you through the pros and cons of cloth nappies and disposable nappies to help you decide.

Changing a nappy: in pictures

Nappy changing in simple pictures: have nappies and wipes handy. Clean baby’s bottom and put on a fresh nappy. Never leave baby unattended on a change table.

Nappy rash

Nappy rash makes children’s bottoms inflamed. To treat it, change nappies often, keep skin clean and dry, apply barrier cream and use disposable nappies.

Tooth decay

Tooth decay is bad news for children and teens. But there’s good news: your child can avoid tooth decay with good dental care and healthy eating and drinking.

Dressing babies for bed

Babies sleep well when they’re not too hot or cold. Dress babies in enough clothes to keep them warm without blankets, or try a safe infant sleeping bag.

Finding a family GP

A good family GP understands the importance of getting to know you and your children. Location, opening hours, fees and availability are important too.

Medicines for children: a guide

Babies: health concerns

Serious childhood illnesses: 0-3 years

Symptoms of serious illness in babies and young children include severe drowsiness, breathing difficulty, blue skin, seizures, fever and frequent vomiting.

Fever

If your child’s temperature is higher than 38°C, it’s probably a fever. A fever is a sign of illness. Here’s what to do when your child has a fever.

Taking your child’s temperature

Digital probe thermometers are the most accurate way to check your child’s temperature. You can also use digital ear or forehead thermometers. Find out more.

COVID-19 and children in Australia

For most children, COVID-19 causes mild flu-like symptoms. Children should do a COVID test if they have symptoms. You can manage mild COVID symptoms at home.

Colds

A cold can make your child feel miserable. There’s no cure, but you can treat cold symptoms so your child feels more comfortable. Fluids are important.

Middle ear infection

Children with middle ear infections usually have pain or discomfort in the ear. If you think your child has a middle ear infection, it’s best to see a GP.

Second-hand smoke and third-hand smoke: effects on children

Second-hand smoke and third-hand smoke can cause serious health problems for children. To reduce the risk, quit smoking and have a smoke-free home and car.

Cradle cap

Cradle cap is the oily, scaly crust that babies get on scalps and torsos and in body folds. It usually doesn’t need treatment and goes away by itself.

Swimming pool hygiene

Sometimes germs in swimming pools cause gastro and ear, eye, skin and chest infections. Swimming pool hygiene helps your family stay healthy in pool water.

Circumcision

Experts agree that the risks of circumcision for boys for non-medical reasons outweigh benefits. It’s important to make an informed choice about circumcision.

Babies: allergies & intolerances

Allergies in children and teenagers

Allergies in children – what are they? And what can you do about them? This guide explains causes and treatments for allergies in children and teenagers.

Non-food allergies in children and teenagers

Children should avoid non-food items that give them allergies, like chemicals, dust mites, grasses, weeds or trees, insect bites, latex, medicines and pets.

Food allergies in children and teenagers

If your child has food allergies, your child must avoid the foods that they’re allergic to. You need to know the symptoms and how to treat an allergic reaction.

Food intolerances in children and teenagers

Food intolerance symptoms in children and teens include bloating, diarrhoea and stomach pain. If you think your child has food intolerance, talk to your GP.

Lactose intolerance: babies, children and teenagers

Lactose intolerance symptoms include wind, stomach pain, bloating and diarrhoea. Babies might also have nappy rash. Your child should see the GP.

Babies: mental health & wellbeing

Mental health for babies and toddlers

Good mental health is important for development and wellbeing. Relationships, play and a safe environment support good mental health in babies and toddlers.
